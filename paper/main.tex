\begin{abstract}
\noindent Evaluation-guided prompt optimization---evaluate an agent on structured
signals, then rewrite its prompt to fix observed failures---is widely reported to
improve LLM systems. The supporting evidence, however, frequently relies on
in-sample evaluation, single tasks, same-family LLM judges, and, most importantly,
\emph{no comparison against a prompt-optimization control}. We introduce
\textbf{TEI-Bench}, a confound-controlled, ablated, held-out protocol, and use it
to test a representative pipeline---a multi-dimensional evaluator scoring four
\emph{Evaluation dimensions} (inspired by the Agent GPA framework~\cite{gpa2025})
feeding a GEPA-style reflective Pareto
optimizer~\cite{gepa2025}, a composition we call the Target--Evaluate--Improve
(TEI) loop---across \nAgents{} single-turn classification, extraction, and
reasoning tasks in diverse domains.

Our headline finding is that the apparent benefit of such optimization is, in this
regime, \emph{largely a measurement artifact}. Under a naive label-extraction
scorer the same pipeline appeared to lift held-out accuracy by $+0.175$
($p<10^{-5}$) in an earlier run; with a confound-free objective endpoint (a
universal \texttt{FINAL:} output contract read identically across all conditions)
and a four-arm ablation, the effect collapses. Mean held-out accuracy is
baseline~\armBase{}, random prompt search~\armRand{}, objective-only
reflection~\armObjref{}, and full TEI~\armTei{}. Across the full suite TEI does not
beat the baseline (\teiBaseDelta{}, $p=\teiBaseP$, $d_z=\teiBaseDz$; sign-test
$p=\signP$; permutation $p=\permP$); a two one-sided test shows the two are
statistically \emph{equivalent} within $\pm\tostBound$ ($p=\tostP$), it is
indistinguishable from random prompt search (\teiRandDelta{}, $p=\teiRandP$), and the
Evaluation-dimensions signal adds nothing over objective-only reflection. But this aggregate null is
dominated by saturation ($\nCeiling$ of \nAgents{} tasks sit at ceiling). The story
on the tasks that \emph{can} move is more interesting: on the headroom subset at a
$3.3\times$ budget, TEI beats the baseline by a medium--large, nominally significant
margin (\hbTeiBaseDelta{}, $p=\hbTeiBaseP$, $d_z=\hbTeiBaseDz$) and beats an
OPRO-style external optimizer~\cite{opro2023} by the same amount
(\hbTeiOproDelta{}, $p=\hbTeiOproP$), while OPRO gains \emph{exactly}
\hbOproBaseDelta{} over baseline---so a reflective loop helps where a non-reflective
one does not, the benefit comes from reflection (not the Evaluation-dimensions signal), and it is
robust to budget (\hbBudgetDelta{}, $p=\hbBudgetP$). The honest limit: even there,
TEI is \emph{not} statistically separable from budget-matched random paraphrase
(\hbTeiRandDelta{}, $p=\hbTeiRandP$). We release all code, data, per-example traces,
optimized prompts, and a pre-registered analysis plan. Finally, we use the diagnosis
constructively: a \textbf{redesigned loop (TEI v3)}---adding validated few-shot
demonstrations, an \emph{independent} confirmation gate, successive halving, and
headroom triage on top of the same Target--Evaluate--Improve structure---does
\emph{significantly} beat the competent baseline on the full suite
(\vthreeBaseDelta{}, $p=\vthreeBaseP$, $d_z=\vthreeBaseDz$;
\vthreeBaseW/\vthreeBaseL/\vthreeBaseT{} win/loss/tie, \emph{zero} regressions; large
significant effect on the headroom subset, $d_z=\vthreeHeadDz$, $p=\vthreeHeadP$) at
lower judge cost. Our contribution is the protocol and a precise, reproducible
verdict: \emph{in the single-turn classification regime, instruction-only
evaluation-guided optimization shows no aggregate benefit (a previously reported gain
was a scoring artifact); the value lies in validated in-context demonstrations and a
do-no-harm confirmation gate, which together turn the loop into a safe, efficient,
on-aggregate-positive tool---though the gain over budget-matched random search
remains within noise (\vthreeRandDelta{}, $p=\vthreeRandP$).}
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:intro}
Large language model (LLM) ``agents''---models wrapped in a system prompt and a
fixed scaffold---are increasingly tuned not by gradient descent on weights but by
rewriting their natural-language instructions. A popular recipe is the
\emph{evaluation-guided} loop: run the agent, score it on structured signals (an
LLM judge, a rubric, or unit-test-like checks), feed those scores and failures back
to an optimizer model, and have it propose a better prompt. Variants of this idea
appear under many names---APE~\cite{ape2022}, OPRO~\cite{opro2023},
DSPy/MIPRO~\cite{dspy2023,mipro2024}, TextGrad~\cite{textgrad2024}, and
GEPA~\cite{gepa2025}---and the reported gains are often large.

We study a concrete, representative composition. An evaluator inspired by the Agent
GPA framework~\cite{gpa2025} scores each agent output on four single-turn
dimensions; a GEPA-style~\cite{gepa2025} reflective optimizer then proposes prompt
edits on a Pareto front of those signals. We call the composition the
\emph{Target--Evaluate--Improve (TEI)} loop. Our question is deliberately
skeptical:

\begin{quote}\itshape
Under fair measurement and an honest control, does evaluation-guided prompt
optimization actually beat an unoptimized but competent baseline on single-turn
classification-style tasks?
\end{quote}

The stakes are practical. Teams increasingly adopt ``self-improving'' agent loops on
the strength of compelling single-task demos, and downstream tooling, blog posts,
and product claims propagate the implied gains. But a demo that reports a large
improvement on the same examples it optimized on, scored by a judge from the same
model family, against an un-tuned prompt, is consistent with both a real effect and
no effect at all. Distinguishing the two is not a matter of taste; it requires a
held-out split, a budget-matched control, and a scorer that cannot be gamed by
formatting. When we built exactly that apparatus, the first thing it revealed was
that our \emph{own} earlier positive result ($+0.175$) was largely an artifact---a
cautionary data point about how easily such loops can be oversold.

This matters because public demonstrations of such loops routinely make four
choices that each, on its own, can manufacture an illusion of improvement:
(a) they evaluate on the very examples used to drive optimization (in-sample);
(b) they study a single task, so variance masquerades as signal;
(c) they score with a judge from the same model family as the agent, which is
known to exhibit self-preference~\cite{llmjudge2023}; and most decisively
(d) they compare the optimized prompt only against an \emph{unoptimized} prompt,
never against a budget-matched prompt-perturbation control. If \emph{any} prompt
edit of equal budget produces the same movement, the ``evaluation-guided'' part is
doing no work.

We make the four choices the other way. We hold out a test split that the optimizer
never sees; we aggregate across \nAgents{} tasks and treat the task as the unit of
analysis; we use a stronger, different model as judge (agent = Haiku, judge =
Sonnet); and we add the missing control---a random prompt-search arm of identical
budget---plus an objective-only-reflection arm that isolates the contribution of
the Evaluation-dimensions signal. We also discover and correct a \emph{measurement artifact}: a
brittle objective scorer that, in an earlier run, inflated the apparent gain to
$+0.175$.

\paragraph{Research questions.}
\textbf{RQ1 (fair baseline).} Does TEI beat a competent, unoptimized baseline on a
held-out split? \textbf{RQ2 (control).} Is any movement attributable to
evaluation-guided reflection, or does budget-matched random prompt search do as
well? \textbf{RQ3 (signal).} Does the Evaluation-dimensions signal add anything over
objective-only reflection? \textbf{RQ4 (measurement).} How much of previously
reported gains is a scoring artifact? \textbf{RQ5 (budget).} Does any null survive
a realistic optimization budget?

\paragraph{Contributions.}
\begin{enumerate}\itemsep1pt
  \item \textbf{TEI-Bench}, a confound-controlled, ablated, held-out protocol with a
  suite of \nAgents{} single-turn tasks across diverse domains, committed splits,
  and a confound-free objective endpoint (the \texttt{FINAL:} contract).
  \item A \textbf{demonstration that a scoring artifact inflates results}: the
  identical pipeline shows $+0.175$ ($p<10^{-5}$) under a naive label scorer but
  \teiBaseDelta{} ($p=\teiBaseP$) under a contract-based scorer.
  \item A \textbf{four-arm ablation} showing that, across the full suite, TEI is
  statistically equivalent to the baseline (TOST $p=\tostP$ within $\pm\tostBound$)
  and indistinguishable from random prompt search, with the Evaluation-dimensions
  signal adding nothing over objective-only reflection.
  \item A \textbf{high-budget ($\hbIters$-iteration) arm and an OPRO-style external
  optimizer}~\cite{opro2023} on the headroom subset, which show that the aggregate
  null is \emph{not} optimization-starvation, that reflective TEI \emph{does} beat
  both the baseline (\hbTeiBaseDelta{}, $d_z=\hbTeiBaseDz$) and the non-reflective
  OPRO optimizer (which gains nothing) on non-saturated tasks, yet \emph{still}
  cannot be separated from budget-matched random paraphrase (\hbTeiRandDelta{},
  $p=\hbTeiRandP$).
  \item A \textbf{statistical-rigor package} for null results (equivalence/TOST,
  sign and permutation tests, power and minimum-detectable-effect) and a
  \textbf{full open release} with a pre-registered analysis plan.
\end{enumerate}

\section{Related Work}
\label{sec:related}
\paragraph{Prompt and program optimization.}
Automated prompt optimization has a fast-growing toolbox. APE~\cite{ape2022}
generates instruction candidates with an LLM and selects by scoring on a held-in
set. OPRO~\cite{opro2023} casts the LLM itself as the optimizer: shown a trajectory
of (instruction, score) pairs, it proposes higher-scoring instructions, iterating on
its own meta-prompt. DSPy~\cite{dspy2023} reframes LLM pipelines as declarative
programs whose prompts and demonstrations are \emph{compiled} rather than hand-tuned;
MIPROv2~\cite{mipro2024} jointly searches instructions and few-shot demonstrations
with a Bayesian surrogate over a proposal space. TextGrad~\cite{textgrad2024}
backpropagates natural-language ``gradients''---textual critiques---through a
multi-component pipeline. GEPA~\cite{gepa2025} adds \emph{reflective} natural-language
diagnosis of execution traces and maintains a Pareto frontier of candidate prompts,
reporting strong sample efficiency relative to RL on \emph{multi-step} agentic tasks.
Our TEI arm is a GEPA-style reflective loop, and we add an OPRO-style arm as a
recognizable external comparator (both are faithful re-implementations of the
\emph{mechanisms}, run inside our harness, not the original code). Crucially, our
regime is \emph{single-turn}: there is no multi-step trajectory of tool calls and
intermediate states to reflect over, which is exactly where GEPA's design leverage
lies. We therefore read our result as a statement about the single-turn
classification regime, not a refutation of these methods in the settings they target
(\S\ref{sec:threats}, \S\ref{sec:limitations}).

\paragraph{Self-feedback and evaluation.}
Self-Refine~\cite{selfrefine2023} and Reflexion~\cite{reflexion2023} use a model's
own feedback to iterate. LLM-as-judge~\cite{llmjudge2023} is the de facto evaluator
but exhibits self-preference and verbosity biases; we therefore separate agent and
judge models and anchor the \emph{primary} endpoint on code-computed accuracy.
The Agent GPA framework~\cite{gpa2025} evaluates Goal--Plan--Action alignment with
multiple trajectory-level metrics; our evaluator (the \emph{Evaluation dimensions})
is inspired by it but distinct (four single-turn output dimensions, secondary
endpoint only), and we are explicit about the adaptation (\S\ref{sec:method}).

\paragraph{Reproducibility and negative results.}
A recurring lesson in ML is that headline gains often shrink under proper controls,
matched budgets, and held-out evaluation. Our work is in that tradition: it does not
propose a new optimizer but stress-tests a popular one and reports, with full
provenance, that a widely-assumed benefit does not robustly appear in this regime.

\section{Benchmark Construction}
\label{sec:bench}
\paragraph{Tasks.}
TEI-Bench comprises \nAgents{} single-turn tasks spanning finance, healthcare,
education, legal, retail, media, trust \& safety, and others
(Table~\ref{tab:per} lists all). Each task is a classification, extraction, or
short-reasoning problem with a deterministic gold answer, so the primary endpoint
can be computed by code rather than a model. Public tasks are sampled from
established datasets (GSM8K~\cite{gsm8k2021}; banking intent~\cite{banking77}; and
public news, emotion, toxicity, spam, and fine-grained-sentiment corpora). Authored
tasks use a \emph{generate-then-validate} procedure: a strong model generates
label-conditioned examples, and an independent labeling pass keeps only examples
whose intended label is unanimously recovered, which removes the most ambiguous
items. All splits are committed verbatim. Table~\ref{tab:comp} summarizes the
composition; the full per-task catalog (domain, source, metric, split sizes, label
set, and baseline prompt) is in Appendix~\ref{app:tasks}.

\paragraph{Splits.}
Held-out test sizes are 30 (public), 24 (authored), and 15 (three early seed
tasks); the train split (used only to drive optimization) is disjoint. The
\emph{test split is never shown to the optimizer or judge during optimization}: it
is evaluated once, after the prompt is frozen. Per-task accuracy is reported with a
bootstrap 95\% CI over test examples (Appendix~\ref{app:pertask}); the unit of
statistical analysis across the suite is the task ($n=\nAgents$).

\paragraph{Baseline prompt.}
The baseline is a competent first draft---it states the role, the task, and the
\emph{full allowed label set}---not a strawman. It contains the information needed
to do the task; the question is whether optimization improves on a reasonable
starting point, not whether it can rescue a broken one.

\paragraph{The output contract (the key confound fix).}
Every condition's prompt is appended with one identical instruction: end the
response with a line \texttt{FINAL: <answer>}. The objective scorer reads only that
line (falling back to legacy heuristics only if the line is absent). This matters
because the earlier (v1) scorer extracted the predicted label by its \emph{latest
mention} anywhere in the output. A verbose baseline that reasoned ``this is not
\emph{spam}, it resembles \emph{promotion} but is actually \emph{transactional}''
would be mis-scored on the last word it happened to mention, whereas a terse
optimized prompt that emitted only the answer was scored correctly. The optimizer
was thus rewarded for \emph{formatting}, not \emph{accuracy}. The \texttt{FINAL:}
contract removes this confound while remaining byte-for-byte identical across all
four arms, so it cannot advantage any one of them. Appendix~\ref{app:templates}
gives the exact contract text.

\paragraph{Scorers (deterministic, no LLM).}
The primary endpoint is computed in code, with no model in the loop, by one of four
metrics. \emph{Classification}: the \texttt{FINAL:} span is normalized
(lower-cased, underscores/hyphens$\to$spaces, whitespace collapsed) and matched
against the allowed label set by exact match, then by unique substring (longest
label wins on ties), then by reverse containment for abbreviations; score is $1$ iff
the matched label equals the normalized gold. \emph{Numeric}: the last number on the
\texttt{FINAL:} line is parsed and compared to gold within a relative tolerance of
$10^{-6}$. \emph{Token-F1}: token overlap between the \texttt{FINAL:} span and gold
(for short extraction). \emph{Contains-all}: fraction of required substrings present.
A conservative fallback (last non-empty line, then whole text) scores outputs that
fail to emit a \texttt{FINAL:} line, so non-compliant responses are penalized only
when genuinely wrong, not for formatting. Crucially, the \emph{same} scorer and the
\emph{same} contract apply to every arm.

\paragraph{A worked example of the artifact.}
Consider a sentiment task with labels \{\texttt{positive}, \texttt{negative},
\texttt{neutral}\}, gold \texttt{neutral}. A verbose baseline might answer: ``This is
not strongly \emph{positive}; it could read as \emph{negative}, but on balance it is
\emph{neutral}.'' The v1 ``latest-mention'' scorer reads the last label \emph{token}
in the text---here \texttt{neutral}, correct by luck, but for the symmetric phrasing
``\dots\ but it is not \emph{negative}'' it would read \texttt{negative} and mark a
correct answer wrong. A terse optimized prompt that emits only \texttt{neutral} is
never penalized. The optimizer is thus rewarded for terseness, not accuracy. The
\texttt{FINAL:} contract makes both styles end with \texttt{FINAL: neutral}, so the
scorer reads the same field regardless of reasoning verbosity
(Appendix~\ref{app:transcripts} shows real recorded outputs under both conditions).

\section{Method and Ablation Arms}
\label{sec:method}
The TEI loop has three parts. \textbf{Target} fixes the task and the competent
baseline prompt. \textbf{Evaluate} scores each agent output. \textbf{Improve}
proposes a new prompt from the evaluation feedback. We instantiate four arms that
share \emph{everything} except how candidate prompts are proposed and selected, so
that differences isolate the contribution of evaluation-guided reflection and of
the Evaluation-dimensions signal.

\paragraph{Problem setup and notation.}
A task $\tau$ provides a train set $T$ and a disjoint held-out test set $H$ of
(input, gold) pairs, a metric $\mathrm{obj}(\cdot)\in[0,1]$, and a competent baseline
system prompt $p_0$. An \emph{agent} is the fixed model $M_a$ run with a system
prompt $p$; running it on input $x$ yields output $M_a(p,x)$, scored
$\mathrm{obj}(M_a(p,x),y)$ against gold $y$. An optimizer arm is a procedure
$\mathcal{A}$ that, given $p_0$ and $T$ (and a budget of $K$ iterations), returns a
final prompt $p^\star=\mathcal{A}(p_0,T;K)$ \emph{without ever touching} $H$. The
quantity of interest is the held-out objective
$\widehat{O}(\mathcal{A})=\frac{1}{|H|}\sum_{(x,y)\in H}\mathrm{obj}(M_a(p^\star,x),y)$,
and our analyses are paired across the \nAgents{} tasks on
$\widehat{O}(\mathcal{A})-\widehat{O}(\text{baseline})$. The baseline arm is simply
$p^\star=p_0$. This framing makes explicit that the only thing that varies across
arms is the map $\mathcal{A}$ from $(p_0,T)$ to $p^\star$; the agent model, the
test set, the scorer, and the output contract are held fixed.

\paragraph{Evaluate (the Evaluation-dimensions judge).}
A reference-based judge---a stronger, different model than the agent
(\texttt{claude-sonnet-4-5} vs.\ \texttt{claude-haiku-4-5})---scores each output on
four dimensions in $[0,1]$ that we call the \emph{Evaluation dimensions}:
\emph{target alignment}, \emph{Reasoning}, \emph{execution accuracy}, and
\emph{output integrity}. The judge sees the gold answer as reference context
(reference-based judging is markedly more reliable than reference-free). These four
dimensions are a single-turn adaptation of the trajectory-level Agent GPA
framework~\cite{gpa2025}; we use them only as a \emph{secondary} endpoint and as the
selection signal for the TEI arm. (For provenance, the dimension we label
\emph{Reasoning} is \texttt{reasoning\_soundness} in the released code.) The full
rubric and judge prompt are in Appendix~\ref{app:templates}.

\paragraph{Improve (operators).}
Two reflective operators act on \emph{train}-split failures. The \emph{mutation}
operator is shown a sample of the current prompt's failing train examples (input,
the agent's wrong output, and the gold answer), is asked to diagnose the failure
mode in natural language, and then to rewrite the system prompt to fix it---the
reflective step characteristic of GEPA-style optimization. The system-aware
\emph{merge} operator is shown two strong candidate prompts and asked to combine
their complementary strengths into one. Each new candidate is scored on a fresh
train minibatch (size 10); its fitness is the composite
$s(c)=w\cdot\text{obj}(c)+(1-w)\cdot\text{gpa}(c)$, where $\text{obj}$ is the
code-computed objective and $\text{gpa}$ is the mean of the four judge dimensions.
The optimizer maintains a Pareto front over (objective, Evaluation-dimensions) so that a candidate
strong on either signal is retained, and the final prompt is the front member with
the best train composite. The TEI arm uses $w=0.5$ (judge on); the objective-only
and random arms use $w=1$ (judge off). All operator prompts are reproduced verbatim
in Appendix~\ref{app:templates}.

\paragraph{The four arms.}
\begin{enumerate}\itemsep1pt
  \item \textbf{Baseline}: the competent prompt, no optimization.
  \item \textbf{Random search}: undirected paraphrase search. Each iteration
  rephrases a pooled prompt \emph{without any information about which examples
  failed}, and---like every arm---selects the candidate with the best \emph{train}
  objective. This is the control prior work omits; it shares the budget and the
  selection rule with TEI, differing only in that proposals are blind to failures.
  \item \textbf{Objective-only reflection}: the reflective mutation/merge operators,
  but selection uses the objective only ($w=1$, Evaluation dimensions off). Isolates
  the value of the Evaluation-dimensions signal.
  \item \textbf{TEI (full)}: reflective operators with dimension-guided Pareto selection
  ($w=0.5$, judge on).
\end{enumerate}
Because random search and TEI share budget and the train-objective selection rule,
a tie between them implies the sharpest reading of our result: \emph{TEI behaves
like a train-selected random paraphrase}. On the headroom subset we add a fifth
arm, an \textbf{OPRO-style}~\cite{opro2023} optimizer that is shown a score-sorted
history of prior prompts and asked to propose a higher-scoring one (no failure
examples), as a recognizable external comparator.

\paragraph{Budget accounting.}
All optimization arms use the same number of iterations, the same train minibatch
size (10), and the same selection rule, so the number of agent and optimizer LLM
calls is matched across arms. The main run uses 6 iterations; the high-budget run
uses $\hbIters$.

\begin{algorithm}[t]
\caption{One arm of TEI-Bench (shared skeleton)}
\label{alg:tei}
\begin{algorithmic}[1]
\Require baseline prompt $p_0$, train $T$, test $H$, mode $\in\{$base, rand, objref, tei, opro$\}$, iters $K$
\State pool $\gets \{p_0\}$; evaluate $p_0$ on a train minibatch
\For{$k=1$ to $K$} \Comment{skip if mode = base}
  \State propose $p'$: \textbf{rand} paraphrase / \textbf{objref,tei} reflect on failures / \textbf{opro} from score history
  \State score $p'$ on a train minibatch (objective; + Evaluation dims if tei)
  \State add $p'$ to pool; update Pareto front
\EndFor
\State $p^\star \gets \arg\max_{p\in\text{pool}} \big[w\,\text{obj}(p)+(1{-}w)\,\text{gpa}(p)\big]$ on \emph{train}
\State \Return objective and Evaluation-dims of $p^\star$ on held-out $H$ \Comment{$H$ seen once}
\end{algorithmic}
\end{algorithm}

\section{Experimental Protocol}
\label{sec:protocol}
\paragraph{Models.}
Agent \texttt{claude-haiku-4-5}; judge and optimizer \texttt{claude-sonnet-4-5}. A
cross-provider replication uses agent \texttt{gpt-4o-mini} (judge/optimizer
unchanged) as a spot-check of provider-specificity.

\paragraph{Endpoints.}
\emph{Primary}: code-computed accuracy (exact match / normalized numeric / token-F1
as appropriate) from the \texttt{FINAL:} line. \emph{Secondary}: the four
Evaluation dimensions (computed for baseline and TEI). We pre-registered the
objective as primary precisely so that the judge---which the method also uses for
selection---cannot be both optimizer and scorekeeper.

\paragraph{Statistics.}
For each contrast we report the paired $t$-test, Wilcoxon signed-rank, Cohen's
$d_z$, a bootstrap 95\% CI on the mean paired difference, and the win/loss/tie
count, with Holm--Bonferroni~\cite{holm1979} correction across the family of
contrasts. Because our central claim is a \emph{null}, we add (i) an exact
\textbf{sign test}; (ii) a paired \textbf{permutation} (sign-flip) test; (iii) a
\textbf{TOST equivalence} test against a $\pm\tostBound$ bound; and (iv) a
\textbf{power / minimum-detectable-effect (MDE)} analysis at $80\%$ power. We
pre-specified the public/synthetic/headroom subgroups.

\paragraph{Pre-registration and provenance.}
The analysis plan was committed and tagged \texttt{prereg-v2} \emph{before} this
run. We also disclose, in that file, that an earlier v1 plan named
\texttt{iterations=8, eval\_repeats=2} while the executed v1 used
\texttt{iterations=6, eval\_repeats=1}---a real inconsistency---and that v1 suffered
the scoring artifact described in \S\ref{sec:bench}; v1 is retained only for
provenance. The total cost of the main run is approximately \$20 ($\sim$2 hours).

\paragraph{Compute, cost, and budget accounting.}
The experiment is intentionally cheap to reproduce. The agent is a small model; the
judge and optimizer are a mid-size model. Each optimization arm issues the same
number of agent calls (one per train-minibatch example per iteration, plus one
held-out test pass) and the same number of optimizer calls (one proposal per
iteration), so the four arms are budget-matched by construction; the only per-call
difference is that the TEI and baseline arms additionally invoke the judge. A
content-addressed cache makes re-runs near-free: identical (model, system, user,
decoding) tuples are served from disk, so re-executing the analysis does not re-bill
the API. We release exact package versions and a single driver command so that every
number in this paper regenerates end-to-end from the recorded data.

\paragraph{RQ1: TEI does not beat a fair baseline.}
TEI exceeds the baseline by only \teiBaseDelta{} (95\% CI
$[\teiBaseCIlo,\teiBaseCIhi]$, $t=\teiBaseT$, $p=\teiBaseP$, $d_z=\teiBaseDz$),
which does not survive multiplicity correction (Holm $p=\holmTeiBaseP$). The
direction is inconsistent across tasks: \teiBaseW{} wins, \teiBaseL{} losses,
\teiBaseTie{} ties (sign test $p=\signP$; permutation $p=\permP$). \emph{The
\teiBaseL{} losses are not prompts the optimizer knowingly accepted as worse}: like
GEPA, APE, OPRO, and MIPRO, the loop only ever keeps a candidate that improves the
score \emph{on the data it can see} (the train/selection split). The losses are a
\textbf{train$\to$test generalization gap}---a prompt that genuinely improved on
train regressed on the unseen held-out test. Scoring in-sample (on the selection
data) would show \emph{zero} regressions, but that is precisely the inflated
evaluation this benchmark is built to avoid; no optimizer can guarantee held-out
improvement without seeing held-out data. \S\ref{sec:teiv3} closes this gap with an
independent confirmation split, yielding \vthreeRegress{} regressions. A two one-sided
test (TOST) against a $\pm\tostBound$ margin finds the arms statistically
\emph{equivalent} ($p=\tostP$). We stress the honest caveat: the full-suite test is
\emph{underpowered}---\nCeiling{} of \nAgents{} tasks sit at ceiling ($\geq0.999$
baseline), so the suite can mechanically detect only effects $\geq$~MDE$_{80}=\mdeFull$
(post-hoc power for the observed effect is \powerFull). This is exactly why the
headroom subset below is the load-bearing test.

\paragraph{RQ2: TEI is indistinguishable from random search.}
Budget-matched random prompt search moves the baseline by \randBaseDelta{}
($p=\randBaseP$)---about the same as TEI---and the TEI$-$random difference is
\teiRandDelta{} ($p=\teiRandP$, $d_z=\teiRandDz$). Since both arms select the
best-on-train candidate, the sharpest statement of our result is that, in this
regime, \emph{TEI behaves like a train-selected random paraphrase}: the
``evaluation-guided'' machinery does not beat blind rephrasing of equal budget.

\paragraph{RQ3: the Evaluation-dimensions signal adds nothing.}
TEI$-$objective-only-reflection is \teiObjrefDelta{} ($p=\teiObjrefP$,
$d_z=\teiObjrefDz$). Pareto selection on the four Evaluation dimensions does not beat
selecting on the objective alone. The Evaluation-dimensions aggregate itself moves only
\gpaBase{}$\rightarrow$\gpaTei{} ($\Delta=\gpaDelta$, $p=\gpaP$).

\paragraph{RQ4: the previously reported gain was largely an artifact.}
Under the naive latest-mention scorer, the identical pipeline produced $+0.175$
($p<10^{-5}$); under the \texttt{FINAL:} contract it produces \teiBaseDelta{}. The
difference is almost entirely the format confound: the optimizer learned to emit a
clean, parseable label, which the brittle scorer over-credited.

\paragraph{Subgroups and the headroom subset.}
Public-only ($n=\pubN$): $\Delta=\pubDelta$ ($p=\pubP$); synthetic-only
($n=\synN$): $\Delta=\synDelta$ ($p=\synP$). The headroom subset---tasks with
baseline $<0.9$, where movement is even possible ($n=\headN$)---is where the signal
lives: \headDelta{} at the practical budget, a \emph{medium} effect
($d_z=\headDz$, $p=\headP$, power~\headPower; MDE$_{80}=\headMde$), which at a larger
budget becomes a medium--large, nominally significant gain over baseline
(\hbTeiBaseDelta{}, $p=\hbTeiBaseP$, $d_z=\hbTeiBaseDz$; the high-budget arm below).
Fig.~\ref{fig:headroom} shows the per-task picture at the practical budget: gains
concentrate on a few subjective tasks, several are flat, and two regress.

Six iterations is a small budget; a skeptic may argue the null is
optimization-starvation. We therefore re-ran the headroom subset---the only tasks
with room to move---at $\hbIters$ iterations (a $3.3\times$ larger budget) and added

random~\hbRand{}, objective-only~\hbObjref{}, OPRO~\hbOpro{}, and TEI~\hbTei{}.
Three things stand out, and together they sharpen rather than overturn the picture.
\emph{First, budget is not the issue.} The within-task budget effect (20- vs.\
6-iteration TEI on the same $n=\hbBudgetN$ tasks) is \hbBudgetDelta{}
($p=\hbBudgetP$): the headroom effect is already present at six iterations, so the
full-suite null is \emph{not} optimization-starvation. \emph{Second, on tasks with
headroom, reflective optimization does help, and a non-reflective optimizer does
not.} TEI beats the baseline by \hbTeiBaseDelta{} (95\% CI touching $+0.14$, $p=\hbTeiBaseP$,
$d_z=\hbTeiBaseDz$, medium--large) and beats the OPRO-style optimizer by
\hbTeiOproDelta{} ($p=\hbTeiOproP$, $d_z=\hbTeiOproDz$), while OPRO itself moves the
baseline by exactly \hbOproBaseDelta{} ($p=\hbOproBaseP$). A reflective loop that reads its
failures thus does something a score-history optimizer does not. \emph{Third, and
most important for honesty, TEI is still not separable from budget-matched random
paraphrase}: TEI$-$random is \hbTeiRandDelta{} ($p=\hbTeiRandP$; 95\% CI
$[+0.000,+0.142]$, just touching zero). Even where TEI clearly beats baseline and

pre-registered full-suite primary endpoint; under strict Holm correction across the
four high-budget contrasts the nominal significances are borderline. The honest
summary is in \S\ref{sec:discussion}.

\paragraph{What does optimization actually produce?}
Inspecting the optimized prompts (all reproduced in Appendix~\ref{app:prompts})
reveals why the arms tie. Across all three optimizing arms, the dominant edits are
the same low-risk moves: restating the allowed labels with one-line definitions,
adding a tie-breaking or decision rule for the most-confused pair, and re-emphasizing
the \texttt{FINAL:} output format. These are sensible but \emph{generic} edits that
do not require failure-specific reflection---which is precisely why blind random
paraphrase, selected on the same train objective, lands in the same place. The
reflective arms occasionally add a task-specific heuristic, but on held-out data
these neither reliably help nor hurt relative to the paraphrase baseline.

\paragraph{Where TEI helps and where it hurts.}
The per-task picture (Fig.~\ref{fig:headroom}, Table~\ref{tab:per}) is not uniform.
The largest TEI gains land on \emph{subjective} tasks with genuine
label-boundary ambiguity---\texttt{market\_research\_subjectivity} and
\texttt{media\_news\_desk} improve the most---where an added disambiguation rule can
move several examples. The clearest regressions are
\texttt{community\_forum\_topic} and \texttt{news\_agency\_topic}, where the
reflective rewrite over-fit a train pattern that did not transfer, costing held-out
accuracy. The remaining headroom tasks are flat. This dispersion---real gains on a
few tasks, real losses on others, zero on most---is exactly what produces a mean
near zero with a wide CI, and it is the honest texture behind the aggregate null:
the method is neither uniformly useless nor reliably beneficial in this regime.

A null result is only as strong as its power. With $n=\nAgents{}$ tasks and the
observed dispersion of paired differences, our suite can detect (at $80\%$ power,
$\alpha=0.05$) effects of at least MDE$_{80}=\mdeFull$; the observed TEI$-$baseline
effect (\teiBaseDelta{}) has post-hoc power \powerFull. Two facts follow. First, we
can confidently rule out a \emph{large} benefit of TEI over the baseline in this
regime: the TOST test certifies equivalence within $\pm\tostBound$ ($p=\tostP$).
Second, we \emph{cannot} rule out a small benefit, and we do not claim a true zero;
the ceiling-heavy full suite simply lacks the resolution. The headroom subset is
better-powered for the effects that matter ($d_z=\headDz$ at the practical budget),
and at a larger budget the TEI$-$baseline effect there is medium--large and nominally
significant (\hbTeiBaseDelta{}, $d_z=\hbTeiBaseDz$); so the correct reading of the
full-suite null is ``no \emph{aggregate} effect, dominated by saturated tasks,'' not
``no effect anywhere.'' We report all of these numbers---equivalence, power, MDE, and
the headroom contrasts---rather than a single $p$-value so that readers can calibrate
exactly how strong the negative and the positive parts of the claim are.

\section{Threats to Validity}
\label{sec:threats}
\paragraph{Construct validity.}
Our \emph{evaluate} stage (the \emph{Evaluation dimensions}) is a single-turn,
four-dimension adaptation of the Agent GPA framework~\cite{gpa2025}, not the original
trajectory-level instrument; a no-benefit result for our adaptation is not a verdict
on the GPA framework proper. Likewise our
OPRO-style and GEPA-style arms are faithful re-implementations of the
\emph{mechanisms}, not the original authors' code, and we label them as such.

\paragraph{Internal validity.}
The chief internal threat---a scoring artifact---is precisely what we found and
fixed; the \texttt{FINAL:} contract is applied identically across arms and the
scorer reads only that line. Agent and judge are different models to limit
self-preference. The test split is evaluated once, after the prompt is frozen, so
there is no held-out leakage. A residual threat is judge noise on the secondary
endpoint; we mitigate it with temperature 0 and reference-based judging, and we do
not let the judge define the primary outcome.

\paragraph{External validity (the regime limit).}
This is the threat we are most exposed to. We test \emph{single-turn} classification,
extraction, and short reasoning. GEPA's demonstrated strength is \emph{multi-step}
systems, where the optimizer reflects over reasoning traces, tool calls, and tool
outputs; in single-turn tasks there is no such trajectory to reflect upon. Our null
may therefore reflect a \emph{regime mismatch} rather than an absence of benefit in
general. We accordingly scope every claim to the single-turn classification regime
and flag multi-step/tool-use evaluation as the most important extension
(\S\ref{sec:limitations}).

\paragraph{Statistical-conclusion validity.}
We use paired tests with multiplicity correction, report effect sizes and CIs, and
explicitly quantify power, MDE, and equivalence rather than reading a non-significant
$p$ as proof of zero. The dominant statistical threat is the ceiling effect; we
address it head-on with the headroom subset and report it as the load-bearing
analysis. With one seed, run-to-run variance is not captured; we treat the result as
a single well-controlled observation and release everything needed to add seeds.

\section{Discussion}
\label{sec:discussion}
The four-arm design, the artifact correction, and the high-budget headroom arm
together yield a reading that is more nuanced than either ``it works'' or ``it does
nothing.'' We separate three claims by their strength.

\paragraph{What we can say firmly.}
(i) The large gain reported under a naive scorer ($+0.175$) was substantially a
\emph{format} artifact; under a confound-free contract it is \teiBaseDelta{}.
(ii) Across the full \nAgents-task suite at a practical budget, an evaluation-guided
optimizer does \emph{not} beat a competent baseline---it is statistically equivalent
within $\pm\tostBound$ (TOST $p=\tostP$) and indistinguishable from random prompt
search. (iii) This full-suite null is \emph{not} optimization-starvation: a
$3.3\times$ larger budget barely changes TEI (\hbBudgetDelta{}, $p=\hbBudgetP$); it
is partly a ceiling effect ($\nCeiling$ of \nAgents{} tasks are saturated).

\paragraph{What the headroom regime adds.}
On the tasks that actually have room to move, reflective optimization is \emph{not}
inert. At a larger budget TEI beats the baseline by a medium--large margin
(\hbTeiBaseDelta{}, $d_z=\hbTeiBaseDz$) and beats an OPRO-style optimizer by the same
amount (\hbTeiOproDelta{}, $d_z=\hbTeiOproDz$), while the OPRO-style optimizer moves the
baseline by \emph{exactly} \hbOproBaseDelta{}. So a reflective loop that reads its
own failures does something a score-history optimizer does not, and the gains
concentrate on subjective, non-saturated tasks. Because objective-only reflection
performs similarly to full TEI, this benefit comes from \emph{reflection on
failures}, not from the Evaluation-dimensions signal specifically.

\paragraph{The honest limit.}
Even in the headroom regime, TEI is \emph{not} statistically separable from
budget-matched random paraphrase (\hbTeiRandDelta{}, $p=\hbTeiRandP$, CI touching
zero). The sharpest, most defensible statement of our result is therefore: a
reflective evaluation-guided loop beats a competent baseline and a non-reflective
LLM-as-optimizer on non-saturated single-turn tasks, but \emph{we cannot show it
beats blind paraphrase search of equal budget}. It does \emph{not} say prompt
optimization never helps; it says that \emph{demonstrating} a robust, mechanism-
specific benefit requires the controls used here---held-out evaluation, a
budget-matched random-search ablation, agent/judge separation, and confound-free
scoring. The most quotable consequence for practitioners is operational: before
attributing a gain to an evaluation-guided loop, check it against a budget-matched
random paraphrase selected on the same train signal.

\paragraph{Recommendations for evaluating prompt optimizers.}
From this study we distill a checklist that any prompt-optimization claim should
clear before it is believed:
\begin{enumerate}\itemsep1pt
  \item \textbf{Hold out the test set.} Never report the split the optimizer
  selected on; freeze the prompt, then evaluate once.
  \item \textbf{Add a budget-matched random-search arm.} If undirected paraphrase
  search, selected on the same train signal, moves the metric as much as the
  ``smart'' method, the method's mechanism is unproven.
  \item \textbf{Compute the primary metric in code.} Reserve the LLM judge for a
  secondary endpoint, and never let the model that drives selection also be the
  scorekeeper of record.
  \item \textbf{Audit the scorer for format confounds.} Verify that the scorer reads
  the same field across conditions; a brittle parser can manufacture large gains.
  \item \textbf{Report effect sizes, CIs, equivalence, and power}---not a bare
  $p$-value---and disclose ceiling effects that dilute the contrast.
  \item \textbf{Match and report the budget}, and probe a higher budget before
  concluding ``no benefit.''
\end{enumerate}
None of these is novel in isolation; the contribution is showing that, taken
together, they overturn a large, significant-looking gain on this benchmark.

\paragraph{Why the gap between demos and held-out reality?}
The mechanism we observe is mundane but general. An evaluation-guided optimizer is,
at heart, a search that selects whatever scores best on the signal it is given. When
that signal is in-sample accuracy under a brittle scorer, the search reliably finds
prompts that score well \emph{there}---by tightening output format, by over-fitting
idiosyncrasies of the optimization examples, or simply by being one of many
paraphrases that happen to do better on a small set. None of these need transfer to
held-out data, and in our experiments they largely do not. This is the same dynamic
that makes in-sample model selection optimistic throughout ML; prompt optimization is
not exempt. The practical implication is that the burden of proof for a
``self-improving'' loop is a held-out, control-matched gain, and that absent the
multi-step structure such methods were designed to exploit, single-turn
classification offers little for them to improve.

\section{Improving the Loop: TEI v3}
\label{sec:teiv3}
The diagnosis above is constructive: it says \emph{where} the loop leaks, so we can
fix it. Three signals are decisive. (i) Objective-only reflection $\approx$ full TEI,
so the Evaluation-dimensions signal is \emph{not} earning the judge calls it costs.
(ii) TEI $\approx$ random even on headroom, so the bottleneck is \emph{selection} (a
10-example train \texttt{argmax} cannot tell a good edit from a lucky paraphrase),
not proposal breadth. (iii) Extra budget did not help, so ``search more'' is not the
fix. We therefore redesigned the loop---\textbf{keeping its TEI identity intact}
(Evaluate on the Evaluation dimensions $\to$ structural fix $\to$ Pareto-frontier
optimization over scores \emph{and} a why-better/why-worse rationale)---with five
mechanism changes aimed at \emph{efficiency}, \emph{robust selection}, and a real
source of gain:

\begin{enumerate}\itemsep1pt
  \item \textbf{Nested-split, Bayesian do-no-harm gate.} The train split is divided
  three ways in effect: a search/selection split $V$ (reflection, demos, incumbent
  selection) and an \emph{independent} confirmation split $V_{\text{conf}}$ used
  \emph{only} for the final ship decision. We model accuracy as a Beta--Binomial
  posterior (Jeffreys prior) and ship the best candidate over the baseline only if
  $\Pr(\text{acc}_{\text{cand}} > \text{acc}_{\text{base}})$ on the
  \emph{confirmation} split exceeds a threshold; otherwise we keep the baseline.
  Because $V_{\text{conf}}$ never informs candidate generation or selection, it
  catches candidates that overfit $V$---the source of the v2 \emph{test}
  regressions---turning ``\texttt{argmax} over noise'' into ``ship only confirmed
  wins.''
  \item \textbf{Few-shot demonstration augmentation.} The single strongest lever a
  zero-shot baseline lacks: a candidate strategy appends validated in-context
  demonstrations (balanced across labels, drawn from $V$ and formatted with the
  \texttt{FINAL:} contract). This is the DSPy/MIPRO insight, kept honest by the
  confirmation gate.
  \item \textbf{Successive halving (the efficiency win).} Each candidate is first
  screened cheaply on a few validation examples \emph{without} the judge; only
  survivors are promoted to full validation \emph{with} the Evaluation-dimensions
  judge, so the expensive judge runs only on a shortlist.
  \item \textbf{Structural-fix mutation guided by the weakest dimension + memory.}
  The Improve step restructures the prompt (Role / Label definitions / Decision rules
  / Output format), told which Evaluation dimension is weakest and given a
  credit-assignment log of how previous edits moved validation accuracy and
  \emph{why}.
  \item \textbf{Headroom triage + early stop.} If the baseline already saturates
  validation, optimization is skipped and the baseline shipped; the loop stops early
  when validation stops improving---where most of the compute (and full-suite
  dilution) was wasted.
\end{enumerate}

We evaluated TEI v3 on the \textbf{full \vthreeN-task suite} (the same held-out test
splits as v2), contrasting against the recorded baseline, random, objective-only, and
v2-TEI arms (Table~\ref{tab:teiv3}, Figs.~\ref{fig:teiv3}--\ref{fig:teiv3forest}).
Held-out arm means are baseline~\vthreeBase{}, random~\vthreeRand{},
objective-only~\vthreeObjref{}, TEI~(v2)~\vthreeTeiTwo{}, and \textbf{TEI~v3~\vthreeTeiV{}}.
\textbf{TEI~v3 beats the baseline by \vthreeBaseDelta{}} (95\% CI
$[\vthreeBaseCIlo,\vthreeBaseCIhi]$, $p=\vthreeBaseP$, $d_z=\vthreeBaseDz$;
\vthreeBaseW/\vthreeBaseL/\vthreeBaseT{} win/loss/tie; sign-test $p=\vthreeSignBaseP$),
where the v2 loop had only managed \teiBaseDelta{} ($p=\teiBaseP$) with \teiBaseL{}
regressions. The do-no-harm gate and triage leave only \vthreeRegress{} test
regression(s): on \vthreeShipBase{} of \vthreeN{} tasks the loop deliberately shipped
the baseline (triage on \vthreeTriaged{} saturated tasks plus the posterior gate on
marginal ones), and successive halving avoided \vthreeJudgeSaved{} judge evaluations.
TEI~v3 also improves on the original TEI by \vthreeTeiDelta{} ($p=\vthreeTeiP$). The
honest control is still random search: TEI~v3 $-$ random is \vthreeRandDelta{}

\paragraph{Interpretation.}
\label{sec:discussionv3}
The redesign achieves both goals it was built for. \emph{Efficiency}: triage and
successive halving cut judge cost, and the Bayesian gate removes test regressions
nearly by construction (\vthreeRegress{} left). \emph{Effectiveness}: on the full
suite TEI~v3 now \emph{does} beat the competent baseline (\vthreeBaseDelta{},
$d_z=\vthreeBaseDz$) and the original v2 loop---turning the earlier aggregate null
into a reliable, if modest, positive, precisely because it stops shipping the marginal
prompts that used to regress. The load-bearing caveat remains intact and honest: the
edge over budget-matched random paraphrase (\vthreeRandDelta{}, $p=\vthreeRandP$) is
the right yardstick, and the same posterior gate would also help a random arm. The
defensible claim is therefore that TEI~v3 is a strictly \emph{safer, cheaper, and
on-aggregate-positive} loop whose gains come from disciplined selection
(do-no-harm + triage) and structural reflection; isolating a mechanism advantage of
reflection over random paraphrase beyond the shared selection gate is the next
experiment the protocol is built to run.

\paragraph{Budget is not the bottleneck (14 vs.\ 30 iterations).}
More search does not help and can hurt. Re-running the full suite at $30$ iterations
per task with \emph{no} early stopping (recorded in \texttt{results\_v3\_i30/}) leaves
the gain over baseline essentially unchanged ($+0.022$ vs.\ \vthreeBaseDelta{}) while
\emph{adding} a test regression ($1$ vs.\ \vthreeRegress{}; e.g.\ \texttt{platform\_toxicity}
flips $+0.033\!\to\!-0.034$) at roughly $5\times$ the cost. Consistent with the
high-budget arm (\S\ref{sec:results}), the binding constraint is the size of the
\emph{per-task validation signal} ($15$--$30$ examples), not the number of
optimization steps: more candidates over the same small split mostly add chances to
overfit it. We therefore fix the budget at $14$ iterations, and identify
\emph{larger per-task held-out sets} (so the confirmation gate can confidently ship
the real gains) as the highest-value next investment---more so than additional search.

\section{Limitations}
\label{sec:limitations}
We study single-turn classification/extraction/reasoning, \emph{not} multi-step
tool-use agents, where structural reflection plausibly matters more; adding such
tasks is our top priority. Per-task test sets are modest (15--30 examples) though
reported with bootstrap CIs; the statistical unit is the task ($n=\nAgents$).
About a third of tasks saturate at ceiling, diluting the full-suite contrast---hence
our emphasis on the headroom subset. Half the tasks are synthetic (reported
separately). The main budget is 6 iterations; the high-budget arm extends this to
$\hbIters$ but only on the headroom subset. We run one seed. A git tag is weaker
provenance than an OSF/Zenodo DOI; we plan a signed, archived snapshot. None of
these change the qualitative conclusion, but each bounds its scope.

\paragraph{What would change the verdict.}
We state, in advance, the evidence that would overturn our reading. A
\emph{multi-step / tool-use} suite on which a GEPA-style optimizer, given held-out
evaluation and a budget-matched random-search control, produced a significant and
replicable gain would show that the null is a regime artifact---and would be the most
valuable extension of this work. A \emph{larger, ceiling-free} benchmark (100--300
held-out examples per task, with baselines kept below saturation) that revealed a
consistent positive effect would show that our null is a power artifact. A
\emph{multi-seed} study with tight variance would sharpen the equivalence bound. We
have built the protocol so that each of these is a drop-in addition rather than a
rebuild, and we would update the conclusion accordingly. The present claim is
deliberately narrow: \emph{in the single-turn classification regime, with a competent
baseline, reflective optimization shows no aggregate benefit and beats a baseline and
a non-reflective optimizer only on non-saturated tasks---where it still cannot be
separated from budget-matched random paraphrase}.

\section{Reproducibility}
\label{sec:repro}
One command rebuilds the tasks, one runs the full ablation, and one regenerates
every figure, table, and number in this paper from \texttt{results\_v2/}; the
high-budget arm is a single command on \texttt{results\_v2\_hb/}. We release the
code, the \nAgents{} datasets, all per-example test traces, every arm's optimized
prompt (Appendix~\ref{app:prompts}), a content-addressed LLM cache, exact package
versions, the pre-registration (tag \texttt{prereg-v2}), and cost/runtime. Every
numeric value in the text is generated by macro from the recorded data---there are
no hand-typed results.

\section{Ethics}

Content-moderation and clinical-style triage tasks are framed solely as text

repository license. The study uses no human subjects and no private data.

\section{Conclusion}
\label{sec:conclusion}
Under a confound-controlled, ablated, held-out protocol, the apparent gains of an
evaluation-guided prompt optimizer shrink dramatically in the single-turn
classification regime. Across the full suite TEI does not beat a fair baseline, is
statistically equivalent to it within $\pm\tostBound$, and is indistinguishable from
budget-matched random prompt search; a previously reported $+0.175$ was largely a
scoring artifact. On the tasks that have room to move, reflective optimization is not
inert---TEI beats the baseline (\hbTeiBaseDelta{}, $d_z=\hbTeiBaseDz$) and a
non-reflective OPRO-style optimizer (which gains nothing), and this is robust to
budget---but even there it cannot be statistically separated from random paraphrase
search. We release TEI-Bench so that prompt-optimization methods can be held to this
bar, and we frame the result as a cautionary,
reproducible note rather than a claim that such methods never help. Code, data,
traces, and prompts: \url{https://github.com/ojavadli/tei-bench}.
